\noindent
This document is the source of truth for the reusable simulator code under
\texttt{cqed\_sim/}. It records the active physics conventions, the low-level
state-preparation, measurement, and spectral primitives that remain inside the
library, the cleaned package boundary versus \texttt{examples/}, and the tests
that directly back the main conventions.

\section{Overview of \texttt{cqed\_sim}}
\texttt{cqed\_sim/} contains reusable simulator code rather than experiment-specific
workflows:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \texttt{core}: Hilbert-space conventions, frames, the universal subsystem-based
  cQED model layer, two-mode and three-mode compatibility wrappers, reusable
  state-preparation helpers, static-spectrum helpers, ideal gates, and
  manifold-frequency helpers.
  \item \texttt{pulses}, \texttt{sequence}, \texttt{sim}: pulse objects, envelopes,
  channel compilation, hardware distortion, solver paths, collapse operators,
  and extractors.
  \item \texttt{measurement}: reusable qubit readout abstractions, physical
  readout-chain models, and measurement utilities that remain backend-agnostic
  and library-oriented.
  \item \texttt{calibration}, \texttt{observables}, \texttt{operators},
  \texttt{tomo}, \texttt{io}, \texttt{plotting}, \texttt{unitary\_synthesis}:
  reusable helpers that still belong to the library surface.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Study code, workflow code, paper-reproduction code, audits, and smoke tests
remain under \texttt{examples/}. The experiment-facing cleanup note is kept in
\texttt{physics\_and\_conventions/experimental\_protocol\_alignment\_note.md}.

\section{Global Modeling Assumptions}
The reusable models in \texttt{cqed\_sim} are effective cQED models rather than
full circuit-quantization outputs. Unless explicitly stated otherwise, the
simulator assumes:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item a reduced-mode description consisting of a transmon-like weakly
  anharmonic mode and one or more bosonic resonator modes,
  \item rotating-wave style effective interactions for the driven control terms,
  \item dispersive-model Hamiltonians when using the dispersive runtime classes,
  \item a Duffing-oscillator truncation for the transmon sector,
  \item finite Hilbert-space truncation for all modes,
  \item Markovian open-system dynamics in Lindblad form when dissipation is enabled,
  \item and piecewise-sampled or analytic control envelopes as implemented by the pulse layer.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Counter-rotating corrections, Bloch--Siegert shifts, full microwave-network
electrodynamics, and full circuit-quantization renormalization are not included
unless they are added explicitly through model extensions or effective fitted
parameters.

\section{Internal Hamiltonian Unit Convention}
Throughout the simulator, internal Hamiltonian coefficients are represented in
angular-frequency units. Equivalently, the code works with \(H/\hbar\) rather
than energy in joules. Thus:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item all static Hamiltonian coefficients are in rad/s,
  \item all rotating-frame frequencies and pulse carriers are in rad/s,
  \item all dissipation rates are in \(1/\mathrm{s}\),
  \item all times are in seconds.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
The evolution equations therefore use the standard convention
\[
\dot{\rho} = -i[H,\rho] + \mathcal{L}(\rho),
\]
where \(H\) denotes the internal Hamiltonian expressed in angular-frequency units.
Whenever this document writes \(H/\hbar\), it is emphasizing this internal convention.
Whenever this document simply writes \(H\), the same convention is still intended
unless explicitly stated otherwise.

\section{Bare, Dressed, and Measured Parameters}
The symbols \(\omega_q\), \(\omega_s\), \(\omega_c\), and \(\omega_r\) must be
interpreted carefully:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Bare parameters} refer to the uncoupled circuit quantities that arise
  before dispersive reduction or effective-model fitting.
  \item \textbf{Runtime model parameters} in \texttt{cqed\_sim} are generally
  \emph{effective dressed parameters} of the chosen reduced Hamiltonian, unless a
  section explicitly says otherwise.
  \item \textbf{Measured frequencies} are experimentally extracted transition
  frequencies, usually obtained from spectroscopy, Ramsey fits, or energy
  differences inferred from the effective model.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Accordingly:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item the runtime Hamiltonian should be compared against measured transition
  frequencies and effective dressed parameters,
  \item bare circuit parameters belong primarily to translation, fitting, and
  analysis layers rather than to the main runtime dispersive Hamiltonian,
  \item and transition frequencies should be computed from energy differences
  within the chosen effective Hamiltonian rather than by reading raw bare
  parameter values directly.
\end{itemize}

\section{Energy Offsets and Reporting Conventions}
Absolute energies depend on frame choice and on the arbitrary choice of zero of
energy. In this repository:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item identity shifts may be omitted from derived forms of Hamiltonians when
  they do not affect dynamics,
  \item rotating-frame Hamiltonians may produce negative excited-state energies,
  \item \texttt{EnergySpectrum.energies} are reported relative to the bare vacuum
  basis state of the selected model,
  \item transition frequencies and observable dynamics are determined by energy
  differences, not by the absolute value of the reported zero of energy.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Thus vacuum-referenced energy output is a reporting convention only; it does not
change the Hamiltonian that was diagonalized.

\section{State Preparation and Measurement Primitives}
The reusable preparation and measurement helpers that remain in the library are
implemented in:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \texttt{cqed\_sim/core/state\_prep.py}:
  \texttt{StatePreparationSpec}, reusable subsystem state specs, and helpers such as
  \texttt{prepare\_state(...)}.
  \item \texttt{cqed\_sim/measurement/qubit.py}:
  \texttt{QubitMeasurementSpec} and \texttt{measure\_qubit(...)}.
  \item \texttt{cqed\_sim/measurement/readout\_chain.py}:
  \texttt{ReadoutResonator}, \texttt{PurcellFilter}, \texttt{AmplifierChain}, and
  \texttt{ReadoutChain}.
  \item \texttt{cqed\_sim/sim/noise.py}: reusable noise helpers such as
  \texttt{pure\_dephasing\_time\_from\_t1\_t2(...)} used by library code and example workflows.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
High-level experiment recipes and canned workflows no longer live under
\texttt{cqed\_sim/}. Guided notebook lessons now live under \texttt{tutorials/},
while repository-side workflow helpers and standalone scripts live under
\texttt{examples/}. These library objects are not a second solver stack. They are
reusable primitives that compose with \texttt{SequenceCompiler} plus
\texttt{simulate\_sequence(...)}.
In particular:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item State preparation may return either a ket or a density matrix. If any
  subsystem specification is mixed, the joint initial state is promoted to a
  density matrix before tensoring.
  \item \texttt{measure\_qubit(...)} returns exact probabilities by default.
  Sampled outcomes appear only when \texttt{shots} is set.
  \item When a confusion matrix is supplied, the code applies
  \[
  \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{obs}} = M \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{latent}},
  \]
  with columns ordered as latent \((g,e)\) and rows ordered as reported \((g,e)\).
  \item If \texttt{QubitMeasurementSpec.readout\_chain} is omitted, synthetic I/Q
  points are still generated from the lightweight Gaussian cluster model.
  \item If a \texttt{ReadoutChain} is provided, the measurement layer instead uses
  a resonator response, optional Purcell filter, amplifier noise, and mixer-style
  I/Q integration. The same API can also report \(\gamma_{\mathrm{meas}}\) and a
  Purcell-limited \(T_1\), and can optionally apply readout-induced dephasing plus
  Purcell relaxation as a reduced-state backaction model.
\end{itemize}

\section{Physical Readout Chain}
The physical readout-chain path models a driven dispersive resonator with optional
Purcell filtering and additive amplifier noise. This section is an \emph{effective
measurement-model convention}, not the primary source of truth for the runtime
Hamiltonian sign of the transmon-storage \(\chi\).

\subsection*{Effective readout-response notation}
To avoid ambiguity with the Hamiltonian-level dispersive coefficients, it is useful
to conceptually distinguish:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item a \emph{qubit transition pull} per bosonic excitation, which appears in
  Hamiltonians such as \(+\chi_s n_s n_q\),
  \item and an effective \emph{resonator pointer-state separation}, which appears
  in the readout response model through different resonator frequencies for different
  qubit states.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
In the current implementation, the same field name \texttt{chi} may appear in both
contexts for compatibility, but they should be interpreted according to the layer
being discussed:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item in the Hamiltonian sections below, \(\chi\) means a conditional qubit-frequency shift,
  \item in the readout-chain section, the same symbol may parameterize the effective
  resonator pointer-state separation used by the response model.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Implemented effective model}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \texttt{ReadoutResonator} stores \(\omega_r\), \(\kappa\), the qubit-resonator
  coupling \(g\), the complex drive amplitude \(\epsilon\), and an optional effective
  resonator-pull parameter.
  \item The steady-state pointer amplitudes are
  \[
  \alpha_{g/e}^{(\mathrm{ss})}
  =
  \frac{-i\epsilon}{\kappa_{\mathrm{eff}}/2 + i(\omega_{r,g/e}-\omega_d)},
  \]
  with \(\omega_{r,g}=\omega_r\) and \(\omega_{r,e}=\omega_r+\chi_{\mathrm{ro,eff}}\)
  in the effective pointer-state model.
  \item The implemented measurement-induced dephasing rate is
  \[
  \gamma_{\mathrm{meas}} = \frac{\kappa_{\mathrm{eff}}}{2} |\alpha_e-\alpha_g|^2,
  \]
  which reproduces the expected small-pull scaling
  \(\gamma_{\mathrm{meas}} \propto \chi_{\mathrm{ro,eff}}^2 \bar{n}/\kappa\).
  \item The Purcell filter follows the standard frequency-dependent linewidth picture:
  \[
  \kappa_{\mathrm{eff}}(\omega)
  =
  \frac{4 J^2 \kappa_f}{\kappa_f^2 + 4(\omega-\omega_f)^2},
  \]
  so the Purcell rate is evaluated as
  \[
  \Gamma_P = (g/\Delta)^2 \kappa_{\mathrm{eff}}(\omega_q).
  \]
  \item \texttt{AmplifierChain} applies linear gain and additive thermal noise
  before mixer-phase rotation and I/Q integration.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Direct tests: \texttt{tests/experiment/test\_readout\_chain.py}.

\section{Sequential Sideband Reset Approximation}
The sequential storage-reset workflow lives in
\texttt{examples/workflows/sequential\_sideband\_reset.py}. It is an example-level
orchestration built on the explicit three-mode dispersive runtime rather than a
second simulator stack. The modeled protocol is
\[
|g,0_r,n_s\rangle
\overset{\text{storage red sideband}}{\leftrightarrow}
|f,0_r,n_s-1\rangle
\overset{\text{readout red sideband}}{\leftrightarrow}
|g,1_r,n_s-1\rangle
\xrightarrow{\kappa_r}
|g,0_r,n_s-1\rangle.
\]

\noindent
For initial \(|e,0_r,n_s\rangle\) population, the helper first applies an explicit
\(e\rightarrow f\) transmon pulse and then performs the readout-sideband dump
before entering the photon-removal loop.

\noindent
This is a controlled effective-model approximation. The readout-assisted evacuation
stage is represented as a driven effective \(g\leftrightarrow f\) red sideband on
the readout mode followed by explicit Lindblad readout loss, not as an autonomous
always-on microscopic transmon--readout exchange Hamiltonian.

\section{Static Spectrum Convention}
The reusable spectral helper in \texttt{cqed\_sim/core/spectrum.py} diagonalizes
the static Hamiltonian selected by the caller and returns a structured
\texttt{EnergySpectrum} result with both raw eigenvalues and vacuum-referenced energies.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item The diagonalized operator is exactly \texttt{model.static\_hamiltonian(frame)}
  for the chosen \texttt{FrameSpec}.
  \item Reported \texttt{EnergySpectrum.energies} are shifted by subtracting the
  energy of the bare vacuum basis state.
  \item The bare vacuum means every subsystem is in level \(0\): \(|g,0\rangle\)
  for the two-mode model, \(|g,0_s,0_r\rangle\) for the three-mode model, and
  \(|0\rangle\) for cavity-only models.
  \item This shift changes only the reported zero of energy. It does not change
  which Hamiltonian was diagonalized or which transition frequencies follow from
  energy differences.
  \item In the lab frame, the low-lying spectrum is the most directly interpretable
  dressed ladder. In rotating frames, excited levels can appear at negative energy
  even though the vacuum remains the zero-reference state.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Direct tests: \texttt{tests/analysis/test\_spectrum.py}.

\section{Hilbert Space Structure}
\subsection*{Two-mode runtime}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Runtime ordering is transmon first, storage second:
  \[
  |q,n\rangle
  =
  |q\rangle_{\mathrm{transmon}}
  \otimes
  |n\rangle_{\mathrm{storage}}.
  \]
  \item The flattened basis index is
  \[
  \texttt{index}(q,n)=q\,n_{\mathrm{cav}}+n,
  \]
  implemented in \texttt{cqed\_sim/core/conventions.py}.
  \item Computational basis labels are
  \[
  |g\rangle \equiv |0\rangle,\qquad
  |e\rangle \equiv |1\rangle,\qquad
  |f\rangle \equiv |2\rangle,
  \]
  and more generally transmon level \(j\) corresponds to Duffing ladder state \(|j\rangle\).
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Three-mode runtime}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item The three-mode extension uses
  \[
  |q,n_s,n_r\rangle
  =
  |q\rangle_{\mathrm{transmon}}
  \otimes
  |n_s\rangle_{\mathrm{storage}}
  \otimes
  |n_r\rangle_{\mathrm{readout}}.
  \]
  \item The flattened basis index is
  \[
  \texttt{index}(q,n_s,n_r)=((q\,n_{\mathrm{storage}})+n_s)\,n_{\mathrm{readout}}+n_r.
  \]
  \item This ordering is implemented in
  \texttt{cqed\_sim/core/conventions.py} and
  \texttt{cqed\_sim/core/readout\_model.py}, and new extractors assume subsystem
  \(0\) is the transmon, subsystem \(1\) is storage, subsystem \(2\) is readout.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Universal model layer}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item The reusable model core lives in
  \texttt{cqed\_sim/core/universal\_model.py}.
  \item \texttt{UniversalCQEDModel} accepts an optional
  \texttt{TransmonModeSpec}, a sequence of \texttt{BosonicModeSpec} entries,
  explicit \texttt{DispersiveCouplingSpec} entries, and generic
  \texttt{CrossKerrSpec}, \texttt{SelfKerrSpec}, and \texttt{ExchangeSpec} terms.
  \item Tensor ordering remains explicit: the transmon is first when present,
  followed by bosonic modes in declaration order.
  \item \texttt{DispersiveTransmonCavityModel} and
  \texttt{DispersiveReadoutTransmonStorageModel} preserve their historical public
  constructors and delegate Hamiltonian and operator logic to the shared universal layer.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Synthesis subspace}
The unitary-synthesis layer retains the same full-space transmon-first ordering,
but restricted subspace bases are explicitly reordered in
\texttt{cqed\_sim/unitary\_synthesis/subspace.py}; for example, a transmon-cavity
block is ordered as
\[
|g,0\rangle,\ |e,0\rangle,\ |g,1\rangle,\ |e,1\rangle,\ \ldots
\]

\noindent
Direct tests:
\texttt{tests/test\_25\_tensor\_product\_convention.py},
\texttt{tests/test\_27\_three\_mode\_model.py},
\texttt{tests/unitary\_synthesis/test\_subspace.py}.

\noindent
The flexible synthesis API now also permits matrix-defined primitives and
model-backed waveform-defined primitives. The waveform-defined path does not
introduce a second pulse convention: it reuses the same \texttt{Pulse},
\texttt{SequenceCompiler}, and runtime simulation stack as the rest of
\texttt{cqed\_sim}. Therefore it inherits the same carrier-sign rule discussed
later in this document,
\[
\texttt{Pulse.carrier}=-\omega_{\mathrm{transition}}(\text{frame}).
\]
Unitary targets correspond to closed-system effective propagators, while
state-mapping targets are the appropriate synthesis-side abstraction when the
waveform simulation includes dissipation or other open-system effects.
Phase-2 synthesis extends that rule in a controlled way: when a user supplies a
unitary target together with open-system waveform simulation, the optimization
objective is evaluated through propagated probe states rather than by extracting
an exact effective unitary from Lindblad evolution. This keeps the physical
meaning aligned with the runtime solver while still allowing unitary-like target
specification in noisy synthesis workflows. Phase-2 robustness features also
sample uncertain Hamiltonian parameters by cloning the user model and
re-evaluating the same synthesis objective under those sampled parameter sets;
they do not introduce an alternate Hamiltonian sign convention or basis
ordering.

\section{Holographic Quantum-Algorithm Conventions}
The reusable holographic algorithms package in
\texttt{cqed\_sim/quantum\_algorithms/holographic\_sim/} is intentionally
system-agnostic. Its core factorization is
\[
|\sigma\rangle_{\mathrm{physical}} \otimes |b\rangle_{\mathrm{bond}},
\]
with the \emph{physical register first} and the persistent \emph{bond register
second}. This ordering is independent of the cQED-specific transmon--bosonic
tensor ordering used by the main simulator runtime.

\noindent
For a dense joint unitary \(U\) acting on
\(\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{physical}} \otimes \mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{bond}}\), the
implemented holographic channel prepares a fixed physical reference state
\(|0\rangle_{\mathrm{physical}}\) by default and uses the standard Kraus map
\[
\rho_{\mathrm{bond}}
\mapsto
\sum_\sigma K_\sigma \rho_{\mathrm{bond}} K_\sigma^\dagger,
\qquad
K_\sigma = \langle \sigma | U | 0 \rangle.
\]
When a right-canonical MPS tensor is used instead, the implementation stores the
MPS matrices \(V_\sigma\) together with the standard channel-orientation Kraus
operators
\[
K_\sigma = V_\sigma^\dagger.
\]
This keeps the user-facing sampler aligned with standard quantum-channel
notation while remaining consistent with the transfer-matrix orientation used in
the holographic report.

\noindent
The package uses \texttt{burn\_in} to mean repeated application of the same
holographic channel before observable insertions begin. This is the repository's
convention for approaching bulk or steady-state behavior in the bond register.
Observable insertions always refer to measurements on the physical register.

\noindent
Direct tests:
\texttt{tests/quantum\_algorithms/test\_holographic\_sim.py}.

\section{Operator Conventions}
The simulator uses the standard bosonic normalization
\[
[a,a^\dagger]=1,\qquad n=a^\dagger a,
\]
and for the Duffing transmon ladder before truncation,
\[
[b,b^\dagger]=1,\qquad n_q=b^\dagger b.
\]

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item The simulator uses QuTiP Pauli matrices without sign changes. Therefore
  \[
  \sigma_z |g\rangle = +|g\rangle,
  \qquad
  \sigma_z |e\rangle = -|e\rangle.
  \]
  \item The core Hamiltonians use the excitation number operator \(n_q=b^\dagger b\),
  not \(\sigma_z/2\). For a strict two-level truncation,
  \[
  n_q = |e\rangle \langle e| = \frac{I-\sigma_z}{2}.
  \]
  \item The shared operator builder lives in
  \texttt{cqed\_sim/core/universal\_model.py}; the specialized wrapper models keep
  the same user-facing operator semantics while delegating tensor lifting and caching
  to that shared path.
  \item Two-mode operators are embedded as
  \[
  a = I_q \otimes a_{\mathrm{storage}},
  \qquad
  b = b_{\mathrm{transmon}} \otimes I_s.
  \]
  \item Three-mode operators are embedded as
  \[
  b = b_{\mathrm{transmon}} \otimes I_s \otimes I_r,\quad
  a_s = I_q \otimes a_{\mathrm{storage}} \otimes I_r,\quad
  a_r = I_q \otimes I_s \otimes a_{\mathrm{readout}}.
  \]
  \item The generic runtime resolves drive operators through
  \texttt{model.drive\_coupling\_operators()}, so the solver no longer hard-codes
  a transmon-plus-one-mode assumption.
\end{itemize}

\section{Hamiltonian Definitions}
\subsection*{Two-mode runtime Hamiltonian}
The reusable two-mode Hamiltonian exposed by \texttt{cqed\_sim/core/model.py}
and assembled by the shared universal model layer is
\[
H_0
=
\delta_s n_s
+
\delta_q n_q
+
\frac{\alpha}{2} b^{\dagger 2} b^2
+
K(n_s)
+
\Xi(n_s) n_q,
\]
with
\[
K(n_s)
=
\frac{\texttt{kerr}}{2} n_s(n_s-1)
+\cdots,
\qquad
\Xi(n_s)
=
\texttt{chi}\, n_s
+
\texttt{chi2}\, n_s(n_s-1)
+
\texttt{chi3}\, n_s(n_s-1)(n_s-2)
+\cdots.
\]

\noindent
\texttt{cqed\_sim/core/frequencies.py} makes the intended transition law explicit:
\[
\omega_{ge}(n)=\omega_{ge}(0)+\Xi(n).
\]

\noindent
This is the canonical runtime sign convention:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item negative \(\chi\) lowers the transmon \(g\leftrightarrow e\) transition
  frequency as storage photon number increases,
  \item positive \(\chi\) raises that transition frequency.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Textbook-to-code translation}
With the active runtime convention,
\[
n_q = \frac{I-\sigma_z}{2}.
\]
Therefore
\[
\delta_q n_q
=
\frac{\delta_q}{2} I
-
\frac{\delta_q}{2} \sigma_z,
\qquad
\Xi(n_s)n_q
=
\frac{\Xi(n_s)}{2} I
-
\frac{\Xi(n_s)}{2} \sigma_z.
\]
Up to identity and storage-only shifts,
\[
H_{q,\mathrm{runtime}} \doteq -\frac{1}{2}\bigl(\delta_q+\Xi(n_s)\bigr)\sigma_z.
\]

\noindent
If one instead uses the common textbook sign
\[
\sigma_z^{\mathrm{text}}=|e\rangle\langle e|-|g\rangle\langle g|=-\sigma_z,
\]
then
\[
H_{q,\mathrm{runtime}} \doteq
\frac{1}{2}\bigl(\delta_q+\Xi(n_s)\bigr)\sigma_z^{\mathrm{text}}.
\]

\noindent
Hence the projector-based runtime convention is fully compatible with common
\(\sigma_z\)-based textbook forms after accounting for the sign choice of \(\sigma_z\).

\subsection*{Comparison with common literature dispersive forms}
Many cQED references begin from a dispersive Hamiltonian of the form
\[
H_{\mathrm{disp}} \sim \omega_s a^\dagger a + \frac{\omega_q}{2}\sigma_z + \chi a^\dagger a\,\sigma_z,
\]
up to conventions, offsets, and sign choices for \(\sigma_z\). In this repository,
the canonical two-level projector form is instead
\[
H_{\mathrm{disp,repo}} \sim \delta_s n_s + \delta_q n_q + \chi n_s n_q.
\]

\noindent
The main advantage of the repository form is operational clarity:
\[
\omega_{ge}(n)=\omega_{ge}(0)+\chi n.
\]
This makes the meaning of the sign of \(\chi\) explicit without requiring the
reader to track a separate \(\sigma_z\) convention.

\subsection*{Transmon anharmonicity convention}
The transmon nonlinearity is written as
\[
\frac{\alpha}{2} b^{\dagger 2}b^2.
\]
For a physical transmon, \(\alpha\) is usually negative. Thus the adjacent ladder
spacing decreases with excitation number, so the \(e\rightarrow f\) transition
is typically lower in frequency than the \(g\rightarrow e\) transition.

\subsection*{Comparison with the synthesis drift convention}
\texttt{cqed\_sim/unitary\_synthesis/sequence.py} uses the same projector-based
dispersive and Kerr semantics as the runtime model:
\[
E_{g,n}
=
\delta_s n
+
K(n),
\qquad
E_{e,n}
=
\delta_s n
+
\delta_q
+
\Xi_{\mathrm{synth}}(n)
+
K(n).
\]
Its transition shift is therefore
\[
E_{e,n}-E_{g,n} = \delta_q + \Xi_{\mathrm{synth}}(n).
\]
Matching the runtime shift \(\delta_q+\Xi_{\mathrm{runtime}}(n)\) gives
\[
\Xi_{\mathrm{synth}}(n)=\Xi_{\mathrm{runtime}}(n),
\]
so componentwise
\[
\chi_{\mathrm{synth}}=\chi_{\mathrm{runtime}},\qquad
\chi_{2,\mathrm{synth}}=\chi_{2,\mathrm{runtime}},\qquad
\chi_{3,\mathrm{synth}}=\chi_{3,\mathrm{runtime}}.
\]
Likewise the cavity self-Kerr contribution is shared directly:
\[
K_{\mathrm{synth}}(n)=K_{\mathrm{runtime}}(n).
\]

\noindent
The compatibility fields \texttt{synthesis\_chi} and
\texttt{synthesis\_chi\_2} in
\texttt{cqed\_sim/analysis/parameter\_translation.py} are therefore aligned
copies rather than sign-converted quantities.

\subsection*{Kerr free-evolution workflow presets}
The example workflow in \texttt{examples/workflows/kerr\_free\_evolution.py}
builds a two-mode storage--transmon model from human-readable frequencies in Hz
and converts them to the runtime's angular-frequency units with
\texttt{physics\_and\_conventions.conventions.to\_internal\_units()}.
Its default frame is
\[
\omega_{s,\mathrm{frame}} = \omega_s,
\qquad
\omega_{q,\mathrm{frame}} = \omega_q,
\]
so the undriven evolution isolates the nonlinear Kerr and dispersive terms after
removing the bare storage and transmon carrier rotations.

\noindent
The Hamiltonian used by this workflow is exactly the two-mode runtime Hamiltonian
evaluated in that rotating frame:
\[
\frac{H_{\mathrm{free}}}{\hbar}
=
\frac{\alpha}{2} b^{\dagger 2} b^2
+
\frac{K}{2} n_s(n_s-1)
+
\frac{K'}{6} n_s(n_s-1)(n_s-2)
+
\left[
\chi n_s
+
\chi' n_s(n_s-1)
+
\chi'' n_s(n_s-1)(n_s-2)
\right] n_q.
\]

\noindent
When the transmon is initialized in \(|g\rangle\), the reduced storage state
undergoes pure self-Kerr evolution; the dispersive coefficients remain part of
the joint Hamiltonian and matter whenever a different transmon preparation is used.

\subsection*{Three-mode storage--transmon--readout Hamiltonian}
The three-mode model exposed by \texttt{cqed\_sim/core/readout\_model.py} and
assembled by the shared universal model layer implements
\[
\frac{H}{\hbar}
=
\delta_s n_s
+
\delta_r n_r
+
\delta_q n_q
+
\frac{\alpha}{2} b^{\dagger 2} b^2
+
\chi_s n_s n_q
+
\chi_r n_r n_q
+
\chi_{sr} n_s n_r
+
\frac{K_s}{2} n_s(n_s-1)
+
\frac{K_r}{2} n_r(n_r-1).
\]
This is intentionally kept in the same runtime convention style as the two-mode path:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \(n_q=b^\dagger b\) is the transmon excitation projector/number operator,
  \item positive \(\chi_s\) or \(\chi_r\) raises the transmon transition frequency
  as storage or readout occupancy increases,
  \item positive \(\chi_{sr}\) raises the storage frequency with readout occupancy
  and raises the readout frequency with storage occupancy,
  \item positive \(K_s\) or \(K_r\) raises the adjacent bosonic spacing by \(+Kn\).
\end{itemize}

\noindent
The model also exposes analytic helpers for basis energies and for the transmon,
storage, and readout transition frequencies.

\subsection*{Optional nonlinear and exchange terms}
\texttt{cqed\_sim/sim/couplings.py} provides explicit builder functions for
\[
\chi_{ab} a^\dagger a\, b^\dagger b,\qquad
+\frac{K}{2} a^{\dagger 2} a^2,\qquad
J(a^\dagger b + a b^\dagger),
\]
and the model classes accept \texttt{CrossKerrSpec},
\texttt{SelfKerrSpec}, and \texttt{ExchangeSpec} entries that are injected into
the static Hamiltonian assembly. This extends the existing dispersive models
without replacing their original API.

\subsection*{Higher-order nonlinear coefficients}
Higher-order \(\chi\) and Kerr coefficients are stored in falling-factorial form,
not ordinary monomial form. For example:
\[
\chi_2\, n(n-1),\qquad
\chi_3\, n(n-1)(n-2),
\]
rather than coefficients multiplying \(n^2\) or \(n^3\) directly. This is a
repository-specific convention and must be preserved consistently in translation,
fitting, and documentation.

\subsection*{Standard-compatible versus repository-specific}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Standard-compatible: transmon-first tensor ordering, QuTiP Pauli signs,
  rad/s and seconds internally, Duffing/Kerr terms, Lindblad dynamics, and the
  canonical rule that negative \(\chi\) lowers the transmon transition with bosonic
  occupancy while positive \(\chi\) raises it.
  \item Repository-specific: writing the transmon term in terms of \(n_q\), storing
  higher-order pulls and Kerr terms in falling-factorial form, and using complex
  envelopes with \(e^{+i(\omega t+\phi)}\).
  \item Needs caution: raw \texttt{Pulse.carrier} remains the negative of the
  rotating-frame transition frequency, so user-facing spectroscopy axes should use
  \texttt{carrier\_for\_transition\_frequency(...)} or
  \texttt{transition\_frequency\_from\_carrier(...)} rather than raw carriers.
\end{itemize}

\section{Drive Hamiltonians}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \texttt{cqed\_sim/pulses/pulse.py} samples analytic or discrete envelopes as
  \[
  \epsilon(t)=\texttt{amp}\,\texttt{envelope}(t)\,e^{i(\texttt{carrier} t+\texttt{phase})}.
  \]
  \item \texttt{cqed\_sim/sim/runner.py} inserts \(\epsilon(t)\) and
  \(\epsilon^*(t)\) as raising and lowering couplings obtained from the model,
  rather than from a hard-coded target list.
  \item Because the runtime drive term is
  \[
  \epsilon(t)\,O^\dagger + \epsilon^*(t)\,O,
  \]
  a rotating-frame transition at frequency \(\omega_{\mathrm{transition}}\) is
  resonantly addressed by
  \[
  \texttt{carrier}=-\omega_{\mathrm{transition}}.
  \]
  The public helpers \texttt{carrier\_for\_transition\_frequency(...)} and
  \texttt{transition\_frequency\_from\_carrier(...)} make that mapping explicit.
  \item User-facing spectroscopy examples therefore plot physical transition
  detuning on the x-axis and convert that value to the internal raw carrier with
  \texttt{carrier\_for\_transition\_frequency(...)} before simulation.
  \item The two-mode path supports \texttt{"cavity"} or \texttt{"storage"},
  \texttt{"qubit"}, and the legacy \texttt{"sideband"} target, plus the structured
  targets \texttt{TransmonTransitionDriveSpec(lower\_level, upper\_level)} and
  \texttt{SidebandDriveSpec(mode, lower\_level, upper\_level, sideband)}.
  \item The three-mode path supports \texttt{"storage"}, \texttt{"readout"},
  \texttt{"qubit"}, and \texttt{"transmon"} targets, plus the same structured
  transition and sideband targets.
  \item The effective three-mode drive Hamiltonian is
  \[
  \frac{H_d}{\hbar}
  =
  \epsilon_s(t) a_s^\dagger + \epsilon_s^*(t) a_s
  +
  \epsilon_q(t) b^\dagger + \epsilon_q^*(t) b
  +
  \epsilon_r(t) a_r^\dagger + \epsilon_r^*(t) a_r .
  \]
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Carrier-sign warning}
\textcolor{red}{\textbf{Warning:}} raw \texttt{Pulse.carrier} is an internal
rotating-frame quantity. It is not the same thing as a user-facing physical
detuning axis. External spectroscopy and plotting code should convert through
\texttt{carrier\_for\_transition\_frequency(...)} or
\texttt{transition\_frequency\_from\_carrier(...)} rather than interpreting the
raw sign directly.

\subsection*{Effective multilevel sideband model}
The reusable sideband path added in \texttt{cqed\_sim/core/drive\_targets.py}
and \texttt{cqed\_sim/pulses/builders.py} is an effective rotating-wave
Hamiltonian, not a microscopic flux-drive derivation. For a red sideband on a
bosonic mode \(a_m\) and a selected transmon transition
\(|\ell\rangle \leftrightarrow |u\rangle\), the implemented operator pair is
\[
\frac{H_{\mathrm{sb}}}{\hbar}
=
\epsilon(t)\, |u\rangle\langle \ell|\, a_m
+
\epsilon^*(t)\, a_m^\dagger |\ell\rangle\langle u|,
\]
with an analogous blue-sideband variant that swaps \(a_m\) and \(a_m^\dagger\).
The public \texttt{SidebandDriveSpec} selects the bosonic mode, the ancilla ladder
transition, and the red/blue branch. The helper
\texttt{effective\_sideband\_rabi\_frequency(g, \(\delta\))} uses the standard
effective-model rule
\[
\Omega_{\mathrm{eff}} = \sqrt{(2g)^2 + \delta^2}.
\]
This path is intended for fast-control and synchronization studies where the
effective manifold dynamics are the quantity of interest.

\subsection*{Kerr-sign notebook diagnosis}
The paper-comparison notebook
\texttt{test\_against\_papers/prl115\_137002\_bosonic\_controls.ipynb} was
audited against the runtime implementation. The core simulator and the documented
physics convention both use
\[
\frac{H_K}{\hbar} = +\frac{K}{2} a^{\dagger 2} a^2.
\]
For the measured parameter set used in the notebook, the runtime evolution
matches the documented negative-\(K\) behavior. The apparent sign flip comes
from interpreting the rotating-frame coherent-state phase trajectory, not from
a flipped self-Kerr term in the Hamiltonian builder. This is guarded by
\texttt{tests/test\_32\_kerr\_sign\_notebook\_regression.py} and exposed through
\texttt{cqed\_sim.experiment.verify\_kerr\_sign(...)}.

\section{Dissipation and Lindblad Terms}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \texttt{NoiseSpec} in \texttt{cqed\_sim/sim/noise.py} supports
  \texttt{t1}, \texttt{transmon\_t1}, \texttt{tphi}, \texttt{tphi\_storage},
  \texttt{tphi\_readout}, \texttt{kappa}, \texttt{nth},
  \texttt{kappa\_storage}, \texttt{kappa\_readout}, \texttt{nth\_storage}, and
  \texttt{nth\_readout}.
  \item The implemented convention is
  \[
  \gamma_1 = 1/T_1,
  \qquad
  \gamma_\phi = 1/(2 T_\phi).
  \]
  \item If \texttt{transmon\_t1=(T1\_ge, T1\_fe, ...)} is supplied, the
  collapse-operator builder emits explicit ladder decays
  \[
  \sqrt{1/T_{1,j}}\,|j-1\rangle\langle j|.
  \]
  If it is not supplied, the older aggregate \(\sqrt{\gamma_1}\,b\) path remains
  available through \texttt{t1}.
  \item Two-level dephasing uses \(\sqrt{\gamma_\phi}\,\sigma_z\) with
  \(\sigma_z = I - 2 n_q\). Multi-level transmon dephasing uses
  \(\sqrt{\gamma_\phi}\,n_q\).
  \item Storage and readout pure dephasing use
  \(\sqrt{1/T_{\phi,s}}\,n_s\) and \(\sqrt{1/T_{\phi,r}}\,n_r\), respectively.
  \item These prefactors are chosen so that the relevant off-diagonal coherence
  decays as \(e^{-t/T_\phi}\) for the intended subsystem convention.
  \item In the two-mode path, \texttt{kappa} and \texttt{nth} still drive storage
  loss and thermal excitation.
  \item In the three-mode path, storage and readout can decay independently via
  \texttt{kappa\_storage} and \texttt{kappa\_readout}, with separate thermal occupations.
  \item The helper \texttt{pure\_dephasing\_time\_from\_t1\_t2(...)} uses
  \[
  \frac{1}{T_2} = \frac{1}{2T_1} + \frac{1}{T_\phi}
  \]
  and clamps the inferred \(1/T_\phi\) rate to zero. Because the helper returns a
  dephasing time rather than a rate, that zero-extra-dephasing case is encoded by
  returning \texttt{None}.
  \item \texttt{simulate\_sequence(...)} uses \texttt{mesolve} when collapse
  operators are present or when the initial state is already a density matrix;
  otherwise it uses \texttt{sesolve}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Gaps Between Code Conventions and Test Coverage}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item The runtime-to-synthesis translation helper exposes synthesis coefficients
  aligned with the runtime convention, but the library still does not force every
  synthesis-side caller to route through that helper automatically.
  \item Higher-order \(\chi_2\), \(\chi_3\), and higher-order Kerr coefficients are
  covered indirectly, but not with isolated sign-and-factor analytic tests of the
  same style now used for the three-mode diagonal-energy checks.
  \item The readout-chain model is physically motivated, but it remains an effective
  resonator/filter/amplifier abstraction rather than a full microwave-network or POVM calibration workflow.
  \item The backend path is dense and piecewise constant; it is intended for parity
  and small-system acceleration checks, not as a drop-in replacement for every QuTiP workflow.
\end{itemize}

\section{Potential Ambiguities}
\textbf{Moderate:} the repository migrated on March 11, 2026 from an older
\(-\chi n_s n_q\) convention to the current \(+\chi n_s n_q\) convention.
Historical notes or saved plots created before that migration may still use the old sign.\\

\textbf{Moderate:} higher-order pull and Kerr parameters are falling-factorial
coefficients, not ordinary polynomial coefficients.\\

\textbf{Moderate:} the runtime writes transmon physics in terms of \(n_q\) rather
than \(\sigma_z/2\), so ground-state energy offsets differ from some textbook presentations.\\

\textbf{Moderate:} \texttt{EnergySpectrum.energies} are vacuum-referenced in the
selected frame, so rotating-frame spectra can contain negative excited-state energies
even though the vacuum is fixed at zero.\\

\textbf{Moderate:} synthetic I/Q output is a convenience sampling layer, not a
calibrated hardware-response model.\\

\textbf{Low:} the explicit complex-envelope sign \(e^{+i(\omega t+\phi)}\) is
repository-specific and must be preserved when adding pulse families.\\

\textbf{Low:} \texttt{FrameSpec} still stores the storage frame in the legacy field
\texttt{omega\_c\_frame}; the \texttt{omega\_s\_frame} alias removes ambiguity but
does not rename the underlying field.\\

\textbf{Low:} the same field name \texttt{chi} may still appear in both Hamiltonian-level
dispersive contexts and effective readout-response contexts for compatibility, so
the surrounding section must determine the intended interpretation.

\section{Summary}
\texttt{cqed\_sim} is a reusable pulse-level cQED simulation library with a
universal subsystem-based model core plus thin compatibility wrappers for the
two-mode transmon--storage path and the three-mode storage--transmon--readout
extension. The authoritative runtime conventions are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item transmon-first tensor ordering,
  \item \(|g\rangle=|0\rangle\), \(|e\rangle=|1\rangle\), \(|f\rangle=|2\rangle\),
  \item QuTiP-standard Pauli signs,
  \item internal Hamiltonians represented in rad/s, i.e.\ as \(H/\hbar\),
  \item seconds for time,
  \item complex envelopes with \(e^{+i(\omega t+\phi)}\) and therefore
  \(\texttt{carrier}=-\omega_{\mathrm{transition}}\) for resonant rotating-frame spectroscopy,
  \item dispersive physics written in terms of \(n_q=b^\dagger b\) with
  \(+\chi n_s n_q\)-style conditional terms,
  \item and Lindblad collapse operators as implemented in \texttt{cqed\_sim/sim/noise.py}.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Negative \(\chi\) means that higher bosonic occupancy lowers the transmon transition
frequency, and positive Kerr coefficients raise adjacent bosonic transition spacings.
Higher-order nonlinear coefficients are stored in falling-factorial form. Runtime
frequencies are effective dressed parameters of the chosen reduced model unless a
section explicitly says otherwise. Vacuum-referenced spectral output is a reporting
convention only and does not alter physical transition frequencies.

\noindent
The experiment-facing layer has two measurement modes: a default lightweight
exact-probability path and an opt-in physical readout-chain path with resonator
response, Purcell filtering, amplifier noise, and reduced-state backaction. The
library also exposes optional nonlinear and exchange Hamiltonian terms, aligned
runtime/synthesis parameter translation helpers, calibration-target utilities, and
an opt-in dense NumPy/JAX backend path.
